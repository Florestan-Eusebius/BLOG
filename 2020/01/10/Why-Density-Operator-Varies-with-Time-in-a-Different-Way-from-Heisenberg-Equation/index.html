To have a better understanding of this problem, let’s study the density operator in Heisenberg picture. Now since the kets do not change, the density operator defined by ($\ref{density operator}$) remains the same obviously (you can also see this by ($\ref{heisenberg time evolution}$) and ($\ref{density time evolution}$)). Does this suggest that the system is always in a state of equilibrium? Of course not! We have known from ($\ref{equation of density operator}$) that in Schrödinger picture there is a condition for equilibrium that $\hat{\rho}$ and $\hat{H}$ commutes, so it is impossible that there is no requirement for equilibrium in Heisenberg picture. To clarify this problem, we need to discuss what “change with time” really means.

For one particle problem, it is the average of an operator on some given state that we care about. The Heisenberg equation of motion concerns how an operator $\hat{A}$, which does not explicitly depend on time in Schrödinger picture, behaves in Heisenberg picture. As for a given set of eigenkets of some other operator $\left|n\right>$, the matrix elements do not change in both pictures.

However, for density operator $\hat{\rho}$, which generally may be explicitly dependent on time in Schrödinger picture, what we care about is the matrix elements. In Schrödinger picture, since the $\left|n\right>$s do not change with time, the condition for equilibrium is that the right hand side of ($\ref{equation of density operator}$) be 0. To summarize, the Heisenberg equation ($\ref{heisenbergEq}$) describes how an operator unchanged in Schrödinger picture changes with time in Heisenberg picture, while the equation ($\ref{equation of density operator}$) describes how the density operator changes in Schrödinger picture. They have similar forms but absolutely different physics.
